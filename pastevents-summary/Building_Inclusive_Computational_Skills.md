<h1>Highlights from WiCS+E March Event: Building Inclusive Computational Skills</h1>

<p>
This year we hosted an inspiring <strong>Women in Cognitive Science – Widening Inclusivity in Cognitive Science (WiCS+E)</strong> March event, with over <strong>80 participants</strong> joining online to discuss gender gaps in coding and modeling, and how to address barriers to improve research methods and foster interdisciplinary careers.
</p>

<p>
Three speakers joined the conversation with different perspectives:
</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ana Paqui Palenciano</strong> (University of Granada)</li>
  <li><strong>Alina Herderich</strong> (University of Graz)</li>
  <li><strong>Cecilia Baldoni</strong> (Max Planck Institute of Animal Behavior)</li>
</ul>

<p>
Through their contributions, the discussion highlighted several key messages aimed at motivating researchers to engage with programming and computational methods.
</p>

<h2>Why This Conversation Matters</h2>

<p>
We began by revisiting a persistent issue: women remain underrepresented in technical and computational roles, including coding and modeling in cognitive science, even though performance differences do <strong>not</strong> explain this gap.
</p>

<p>
Instead, research points to factors such as confidence, self-efficacy, stereotypes, and the learning environment. Supportive teaching and mentorship can make a measurable difference in participation and persistence.
</p>

<p>
This is precisely why this event was organised: to discuss how education, community, and open practices can help close the gender gap and build more inclusive computational spaces.
</p>

<h2>Key Points from the Speakers</h2>

<h3>💻 Do We Really Need to Learn How to Program?</h3>

<p><strong>Ana Paqui’s short answer: Yes.</strong></p>

<p>Programming increases:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Control</li>
  <li>Flexibility</li>
  <li>Automaticity</li>
  <li>Precision</li>
  <li>Transparency</li>
  <li>Reproducibility in science</li>
</ul>

<p>
However, access to programming is not neutral. Stereotypes around “who a programmer is” (male, brilliant, socially awkward, white…) often collide with stereotypes about psychologists (empathetic, caregiving, “soft science”). These colliding stereotypes shape confidence and self-perception long before actual performance does.
</p>

<p>
Therefore, there is a need to <strong>“reprogramme” these stereotypes</strong>.
</p>

<p><strong>Strategies discussed to widen access to programming:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Early exposure: integrating programming and computational skills into psychology degrees (BSc level or earlier)</li>
  <li>Addressing the “elephant in the room”: explicitly teaching about gender gaps and stereotypes</li>
  <li>Diversifying role models in computational teaching and mentorship</li>
  <li>Fighting structural barriers that limit real access to programming training</li>
</ul>

<h3>🧠 Interdisciplinarity in Action</h3>

<p>
Alina Herderich and Cecilia Baldoni shared their personal research journeys combining psychology and computer science.
</p>

<p><strong>Key messages from Alina:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>Finding a project that matters to you helps motivate learning new tools and skills.</li>
  <li><strong>Don’t ask what Computer Science can do for you — ask what you can do for Computer Science.</strong></li>
  <li>Cognitive Science expertise is not secondary — it is essential for meaningful, theory-driven computational research.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Cecilia highlighted additional points:</strong></p>

<ul>
  <li>The <strong>skills vs. self-evaluation gap</strong> is real: many researchers underestimate their skills, especially those from underrepresented groups.</li>
  <li>Get involved in coding and open science communities (online or in person).</li>
  <li>If you don’t ask questions, people cannot help you.</li>
  <li>Don’t wait until you feel like an “expert” — start contributing and learning through participation.</li>
  <li>Sharing the messy reality of code development helps make the field more inclusive.</li>
</ul>

<h2>🤖 What About LLMs and AI?</h2>

<p>
During the Q&amp;A session, we also discussed the growing role of large language models and AI in programming and research.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Yes, use LLMs — but do not trust outputs blindly</li>
  <li>Avoid copy–paste learning when you are starting</li>
  <li>Learn the basics first, then integrate AI strategically</li>
</ul>
